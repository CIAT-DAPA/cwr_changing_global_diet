The Changing Global Diet
Diets around the world have changed dramatically in the past 50 years. Scroll below to learn more about global change in the foods we eat. Click the links at left to explore dietary change in specific countries, compare countries to one another, and learn about the changing importance of particular food crops Crops We studied the changing contribution of 53 crop commodities as well as animal products to calories, protein, fat, and food weight in national per capita food supplies [imagen] Countries
In 152 countries worldwide. To study as much of the world as possible across the entire time series (1961-2009), we standardized areas that changed over the past 50 years, for example by recombining the countries formerly in the the USSR and Yugoslavia And found dramatic change National diets grew larger and richer. On average, countries’ food supplies from both plant and animal sources consistently increased over the past 50 years in terms of calories, protein, fat, and food weight. Oils as a food group showed some of the most dramatic increases
National diets became more diverse
Countries’ food supplies added an average of 9 new crop commodities contributing to calories, 6 to protein, 5 to fat, and 10 to food weight National diets became more even
Countries’ food supplies increased in evenness, indicating greater equality in the relative importance of each of the crop commodities contributing to food supplies within each country The evenness scale refers to Pielou’s Evenness Index, where 0 is a diet totally dominated by a single food, and 1 is a diet where all foods contribute an equal amount The most dominant foods in national diets became less important The proportion of countries’ food supplies comprised of the most abundant crop commodity declined on average. Particularly notable reductions in originally very high levels of dominance were visible in rice in contribution to calories in Southeast Asia, coconut for fat in Pacific Island countries, and groundnut for fat in Central African countries The dominance scale refers to percent contribution to national food supplies by the most important food (whichever it is), where 0 means no contribution to diet, and 1 means 100% contribution to diet
